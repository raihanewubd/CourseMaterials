Summer 2026: Three Project Tracks

In one paragraph. You work in a group on one of three tracks and take it from start to finish over six weeks. Track 1 builds a graph neural network (GNN) on table-style data. Track 2 turns images into graphs and runs a graph convolutional network (GCN). Track 3 builds your own CNN with an attention part for images. Whatever the track, you do the same three tasks: (1) understand the data and read what others have done, (2) build simple baseline models and then your own proposed model, (3) improve it, explain how it makes decisions, and compare it fairly to published work. Your mark is a percentage, read straight off a simple grid (§8). Everything is submitted through one GitHub repository, with a short report at the end of every task. Use the tabs below to move between sections.

1 · Goal and Setting

Each group runs one track end to end. The work should move clearly from understanding the data, to baseline and proposed models, to a final improved model backed by explainable-AI evidence and an ablation study (turning parts of your model off to see what actually helped). Pick your dataset from the companion dataset workbook — it lists approved datasets per section, tagged CORE (use these) or OVERFLOW (advanced / needs sign-off), with the best track for each. State your dataset's source, size, classes, and problem type before any modeling.

The three tracks are separate choices, not stages. Commit to one. The three-task rhythm is the same for all; only the data type, the model family, and the explainability tools change.

Key terms in plain words
  • Baseline — a standard, well-known model you compare your own against.
  • Related work — recent papers (2022–2026) that solved a similar problem; you find five in Task 1.
  • Leakage — when information from the test set sneaks into training and gives a fake-high score. Your job is to prevent it.
  • Graph (Tracks 1–2) — nodes (things) joined by edges (relationships between them).
  • Ablation — turn one part of the model off at a time to see how much it mattered.
  • Explainability (XAI) — showing why the model decided something: which features, or which parts of the image, it relied on.
  • Macro-F1 — a score that averages every class equally, so small/rare classes still count.

3 · Track 1: GNN — Graph Neural Network on Table-Style Data

Pick a processed, feature-based dataset. The main area is EEG (brain-signal features). Also allowed: EMG (muscle-signal) gesture data, and network-intrusion data (e.g. Bot-IoT, TON_IoT, UNSW-NB15) — these already contain a natural graph (sensor channels, or computers-and-connections), which is what makes a GNN a sensible choice. State source, number of samples, number of features, classes, and problem type.

When is a GNN actually the right tool?A GNN helps when a real graph exists (EEG channels that connect, EMG electrodes, network hosts and their traffic). For a plain feature table with no natural links, a simple Random Forest or XGBoost is very hard to beat, and inventing a graph rarely helps — so those datasets make good baselines but weak GNN proposals. You are graded on a justified graph and an honest result, not on forcing a win.

Task 1 — Explore the data + Related work (present 19–23 Jul)

EDA (cover A–H): A summary table (name, source, area, samples, classes, feature count/type, subjects/hosts, balance, missing values, duplicates); B statistics (mean, median, std, min/max, quartiles, skewness, kurtosis, outliers); C data quality (missing %, duplicates, near-constant and highly-correlated features, outliers, and how each affects modeling); D class balance (counts, bar chart, and its effect on metrics); E feature distributions (histograms, boxplots, violin plots; do classes separate?); F correlation (heatmap, redundant-feature list); G 2D/3D view with PCA / t-SNE / UMAP (do classes separate?); H interactive plots (Plotly/Bokeh/Altair) with titles and short readings.
Related work: five GNN papers on your area (EEG-, EMG-, or intrusion-GNN), in the table from §6.3.
Deliverable (GitHub): code/task1/GroupNN_Dataset_task1_eda.ipynb • related_work/ • report/task1/GroupNN_Dataset_task1_report.pdf.

Task 2 — Baselines + your GNN (present 26–30 Jul)

Baselines: several of Logistic Regression, SVM, k-NN, Decision Tree, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost/LightGBM, MLP, 1D-CNN. Preprocessing: subject/host-independent split, scaling, encoding, missing-value and imbalance handling, feature selection; no leakage (§6.2). Baseline evaluation: full metric set (§6.1) + training time; comparison table; name the baseline to beat. GNN proposal: (A) graph construction (nodes, edges, edge weights, node features, and why it fits); (B) an architecture diagram (input → graph → GNN layers → pooling/readout → output); (C) explain it in your own words plus settings. First results: compare to baselines.
Deliverable (GitHub): code/task2/GroupNN_Dataset_task2_baselines.ipynb, ..._task2_proposed_model.ipynb • report/task2/GroupNN_Dataset_task2_report.pdf.

Task 3 — Improve, compare, explain (present 2–6 Aug)

Improve (ablation): vary layers, hidden size, dropout, batch norm, LR schedule, optimizer, graph-construction method, edge weights, GNN variant (GCN/GAT/GraphSAGE), imbalance handling, early stopping. Final performance: final GNN vs. best baseline, first GNN, and related-work numbers (fair, leakage-free — Pillar A, §8). Report 5-fold CV (mean ± std) and a significance test vs. the best baseline (§6.4). Explainability (SHAP + LIME): for one correct and one wrong sample.
Deliverable (GitHub): code/task3/GroupNN_Dataset_task3_improvement_ablation.ipynb, ..._task3_explainability.ipynb • models/ • report/task3/GroupNN_Dataset_task3_report.pdf (final).

6 · Rules for Every Track

6.1 Reporting and figures

Every result must be explained, not just shown. Accuracy alone is never enough: report precision, recall, F1-score, macro- and weighted-averages, a confusion matrix, ROC curves with AUC, and precision–recall curves (use one-vs-rest for multiclass). Every figure needs a title, axis labels, a legend where useful, and a one- or two-line reading of what it shows. Use tables for any comparison between models.

6.2 Data integrity — keeping your scores honest (mandatory)

These are graded. Breaking them costs the affected marks and any bonus.

Do this, or your scores are not trustworthy
  • Split by subject / source, not at random. If several rows come from the same person, patient, recording session, or host, keep them all on one side of the split. A random split lets the model recognise the person instead of the class, which gives a fake-high score.
  • Build the graph after you split (Tracks 1–2). If you build a graph using the whole dataset and then split, test information leaks in through the connections.
  • Remove ID-like columns (network-flow data such as Bot-IoT / TON_IoT / UNSW-NB15): do not feed packet IDs, timestamps, or raw IP/port numbers to the model; they let it "memorise" the trace. Split by time or by host.
  • On imbalanced data, judge by macro-F1 and per-class recall, not overall accuracy.
  • Malware byte-images: they classify well, but Grad-CAM/LIME on them means little (no real "regions" to point at). Say this instead of inventing an explanation.

6.3 Related-work table (Task 1)

Review at least five papers from 2022–2026 in a table. Shared columns: title • authors • year • dataset • application area • method/model • metrics • strengths • limitations • research gap • relation to your work. Track 1 adds the type of GNN; Track 2 adds the graph-construction method and GCN type; Track 3 adds the attention type. These are the numbers you will later compare your own model against.

6.4 Cross-validation and significance testing (required in Task 3)

A single train/test split can win or lose by luck. In the final Task 3 report you must show that your result is stable and that your model's gain over the best baseline is real, not noise.

  • Use 5-fold cross-validation and report each metric as mean ± standard deviation across the folds — not one number. The folds must respect data origin (the same rule as §6.2): group by subject for EEG/EMG, by host or time for network-intrusion data, and stratify for imbalanced image data, so no subject/host leaks across folds.
  • Run a significance test that your model beats the best baseline, using the one that fits your data (table below). Report the test name, its statistic, and the p-value, and state significance at α = 0.05.
Your data5-fold schemeTest to compare two models
EEG / EMG (multi-subject, tabular)Subject-wise (grouped) folds; leave-subjects-out if few subjectsWilcoxon signed-rank across folds (or paired t-test)
Network-intrusion (large, tabular)Time- or host-based split; folds optional (data is huge)McNemar on the large held-out test set
Image, deep CNN/GCN (5 runs costly)Stratified folds if affordable, else one fixed stratified test setMcNemar on the held-out test set; Wilcoxon across folds if you did run CV
Comparing 3+ models at once—Friedman test + Nemenyi post-hoc

Two things students get wrong. (1) McNemar compares two models on the same test samples (paired) — so it tells you whether your model beats your baseline, but it cannot test your model against a paper's reported number (you never see that paper's per-sample predictions). (2) With 5-fold CV you have 5 paired scores, so the natural test is Wilcoxon signed-rank; McNemar needs the raw per-sample predictions on one shared test set, not fold averages. Pick the test that matches what you actually have.

6.5 Understanding your method (Task 2, graded)

In your Task 2 report, explain in your own words (not copied from a paper): what your model does, step by step; why each part is there; and why it should do better than the baselines. A short diagram plus a plain-language walkthrough is what we are looking for. This is Pillar B of the mark (§8) — the four questions there — and is often checked briefly in the presentation.

8 · Marks — how your grade is built

Your mark is a percentage set by two things only: Pillar A, how your final model compares to the best related-work result (fairly, with no leakage); and Pillar B, how well your group explains its own model. Find your row and column in the grid — that cell is your mark. Understanding matters as much as the number.

Pillar A — result vs. the best related-work result

Compare your final model to the strongest of your five related-work papers, on the same dataset, with a fair, leakage-free comparison.

  • Beat — your model scores higher than the best comparable paper.
  • Match — about the same (a small margin either way).
  • Below — lower than the best comparable paper.

Pillar B — how well you explain your model

Four questions. Count how many your group answers clearly — in your own words and correct. A copied, vague, or wrong answer does not count.

  1. You explain the model step by step in your own words.
  2. You justify each design choice (why this graph / attention / layer).
  3. You argue why it should beat the baselines.
  4. You give an honest reading of results — what worked, what didn't, and why.

4 answered = Strong  •  2–3 = Medium  •  0–1 = Weak.

Your mark (the grid)

Result ↓ / Explanation →Strong (4/4)Medium (2–3)Weak (0–1)
Beat80%75%70%

Two rules underneath the grid
  • Completion floor — 40%. Finishing all three tasks honestly, with a leakage-free split, earns at least 40%, even if your model loses and the explanation is thin. Honest effort cannot fail.
  • Leakage void — below 40%. A result that "beats" or "matches" through leakage, an unfair comparison, or an easier dataset scores below 40%, and the floor does not apply. An honest model that loses is worth more than a mysterious model that wins.

Graph bonus (GNN and GCN only)

The graph tracks are harder, so a real, tested graph earns extra — added on the same 5-point steps.

TrackBonusEarned only by
GNN (table → graph)+10%A genuine graph (real nodes/edges), built after the split, with an ablation showing it actually helped — not for merely being on the track.
GCN (image → graph)+5%Same test: a real, justified graph with an ablation that shows its effect.
CNN + Attention0No graph, so no graph bonus.

The bonus is in percentage points, capped at 80%. A Below-result group cannot be bonused past 55% — the bonus rewards a hard, working graph, it does not rescue a weak result.

Worked examples
  • Beat + Strong = 80%.
  • Match + Strong = 65%; with a real GCN graph +5 = 70%.
  • Beat + Weak = 70%; with a real GNN graph +10 → capped at 80%.
  • Below + Medium = 45%; honest completion keeps you at or above the 40% floor.
  • "99%" from a leaked split → Pillar A voided, below 40%.
